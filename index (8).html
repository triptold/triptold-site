Investor Overview · Confidential

The wrong creator makes content.
The right one makes people want to be there.

Triptold connects boutique travel brands with the creators their guests already trust, matched by emotional territory, not follower count. We brief them properly, run the collaboration end to end, and log every decision. That log becomes the asset.

The Problem

Boutique hospitality does not have a discovery problem. It has a trust problem.

There are hundreds of creator databases, Traackr, Aspire, GRIN, Modash, all optimised for reach and follower counts. None of them tells a small, distinctive place which voice will actually make someone book. Get it wrong and you have spent budget on content nobody acts on.

From the industry
"It is not about their reach. Do you want 600,000 Joannas from Love Island in your property? The answer is not to put somebody who has never met you, who knows nothing about you, who does not understand what you stand for, just because they have a number next to their name."
Nicole Antonio-Gadsdon, Founder, Banana Pepper HR  ·  Author, Help Wanted
An Example

A canal-side lodge does not need a 200k travel influencer.

It needs a nature journaller with 11,000 devoted followers, or a walker whose audience has been through something and trusts her completely. The right person does not advertise the place. They make their audience recognise themselves in it. That is the difference between content that gets likes and a story that gets a booking. You can walk through exactly this, a real brief for a real property, in the Demo.

The Solution

An editorial layer on top of a creator network.

Human judgement, a structured brief in the brand's own voice, and a reciprocal pitch where creators propose what they would genuinely make, rather than quoting a rate card. Every collaboration is paid, tracked to real bookings, and logged. The matching gets smarter and more defensible every time it runs. Triptold does not set creator prices: the creator sets their own fee, and Triptold takes a transparent margin on top.

Why Invest

A deliberately small business building an asset a buyer cannot rebuild.

The market is real and growing: roughly $2.7B in global travel influencer marketing, growing around 15% a year. Triptold is not chasing a slice of that with volume. It runs a small number of excellent collaborations that fund the business toward self-sustaining, and build the one thing a competitor cannot copy by rebuilding the platform: the decision log, a proprietary record of which voices move which audiences toward which places, linked to real booking outcomes.

Category Map

Why has nobody done this already?

Four categories sit close to Triptold. None of them occupy the space Triptold is built for, and the reasons why are structural, not accidental.

A
Generic creator marketplaces
Traackr, Aspire, GRIN, Modash, Captiv8. Enterprise-scale, built for transaction volume and cross-platform discovery. Evolusen is the closest of these to Triptold, but it remains a marketplace: brands post, creators apply, no editorial layer, no reciprocal pitch.
B
Travel-specific creator networks
Narrower than the generic platforms but the same marketplace logic, filtered to travel. Still no curation layer, still paid-acquisition-driven, still organised around volume of creators rather than fit.
C
Boutique travel media with brand storytelling
Magazines and content studios with genuine editorial voice, telling stories about brands. They have the voice but not the creator network, the matching mechanic, or a commercial structure that pays creators and brands through a platform.
D
Travel PR and content agencies
Closest in spirit: relationship-based, bespoke, curated introductions. But agencies do not scale, do not compound (no decision log), and charge retainer or project fees rather than running a platform that pays everyone through structured collaboration.
The white space

The intersection none of the four occupy: editorial curation, like the agencies and boutique media, combined with a creator network and structured matching, like the marketplaces, combined with a commercial model that pays everyone through the platform, unlike the agencies, combined with compounding data, unlike all four. Large hospitality groups and enterprise platforms are competing for project budget and brand attention, but not to own the curation layer itself. That layer is not a feature that can be added to an enterprise platform. It is the entire product, and enterprise platforms are organised around the opposite principle: volume. Triptold can define, name and own this category.

Onboarding as Editorial Act

Nobody fills in a form. Everybody answers questions.

When a brand or a creator joins Triptold, the output is not a database record. It is a piece of editorial copy that becomes their profile and travels into every brief in their own voice.

For brands

"What is the one moment that captures what staying here is like?"

"Who is genuinely not right for this place?"

"What is the most generous thing you can offer a storyteller, beyond the room?"

For creators

"What is your obsession?"

"Who should read, watch or listen to you?"

"What kind of brief brings the best out of you?"

Follower counts and audience data are collected, but never lead. A creator profile reads like a writer's bio. A brand profile reads like an editor's introduction. This is the first signal, before any brief is exchanged, that Triptold is not a marketplace.

The Model: how the money works, and what it costs to run
How Triptold Makes Money

The creator sets the fee. Triptold takes a margin, and builds an asset.

Triptold does not set creator prices. Each creator sets their own fee, guided by a suggested rate card. Triptold adds a transparent margin on top, earns an ongoing share of the bookings each collaboration drives, and layers in modest membership and content revenue. None of it is a pay-for-everything platform fee: brands explore freely and pay once, at the point of real value. Every collaboration also feeds the decision log, which is the asset an acquirer ultimately pays for.

The revenue layers

Five layers, in order of how much they matter. The first two are the per-collaboration economics; the next two are the recurring and additional streams that bring the business to self-sustaining; the fifth is where the investor return actually lives.

A typical collaboration earns Triptold roughly £1,246: the embedded margin (~£508) plus the tracked-link commission (~£588, which keeps paying as the collaboration converts bookings over its attribution window) plus a small round fee (~£150). The creator always sets their own fee; the rate card is only a guide. The tracked-link layer is not a "nice extra", it is roughly half the per-collaboration economics, and it is also the outcome data that makes the decision log predictive and therefore valuable to a buyer.

Crucially, none of this is a pay-for-everything SaaS model. Brands explore the network, see the shortlist and the reasoning for free, and pay only at the point of real value. The additional streams (podcast sponsorship, power hours, creator webinars, opt-in premium reporting) are charged for value delivered at the point of use, never as tolls for access.

What SEIS means in practice
UK taxpayers can claim 50% income tax relief on qualifying investments. A £50,000 investment costs £25,000 after relief, so the real cash at risk is about half the headline. Qualifying gains on exit are exempt from Capital Gains Tax, and loss relief cushions the downside. Because the SEIS per-investor limit is £200k a year, the £250k is structured as a lead plus a small number of co-investors.

What this funds: the self-funded pilot.

~8 collaborations funded by Triptold. We pay the creators ourselves (about £16k) to build proven case studies. Brands pay nothing in the pilot. The pilot's product is evidence, not revenue.

Tracked-link attribution live. Proving that a Triptold collaboration drives real, attributable bookings, the proof brands need before they pay, and the outcome data that makes the decision log valuable.

Stories That Travel podcast and editorial launched, building the owned audience and inbound demand. This is the marketing: earned, not bought.

Founders paid modestly (~£45k each) so the team can actually deliver, not defer their lives for three years.

What this deliberately is not.

Not a revenue-multiple scale-up. We will run a deliberately small number of excellent collaborations (tens, not hundreds a year). The return comes from the asset, not from collaboration volume.

Not a pay-for-everything platform. Brands explore freely and pay at the point of value. Trust cannot be bought or paywalled.

The Asset You Are Investing In

The decision log is the thing an acquirer buys.

Every collaboration is logged with the editorial reasoning behind it (why this voice, this place, this audience), linked to the booking outcome via tracked links. A few hundred richly reasoned, outcome-linked matches is a proprietary dataset no competitor has, because nobody else captures the why and ties it to bookings. The collaboration business funds itself toward break-even and builds this asset; the asset is where the return lives. It pays twice: the same intelligence that makes a small team able to deliver more collaborations is what a buyer pays for.

The Planned Follow-On

~£150k, after the pilot proves the model.

Because the founders are paid and the exit is 3 to 5 years out, the £250k SEIS alone does not bridge to a self-sustaining business. A modest follow-on of around £150k, decided only after the pilot proves the collaboration economics, bridges the gap. It is sized to need, not to scale, which keeps dilution small, and it is offered first to existing investors, the warmest, best-informed money, at the most attractive possible moment: when the proof exists. Two ways to structure it, to be decided nearer the time:

Option A · Standard dilution
The £150k creates new shares; everyone dilutes proportionally. SEIS investors move from 15% to about 14%. Simple and conventional.
Option B · Protect the SEIS investors
Fund the follow-on from the spare option pool plus a small founder give-up (about 2% each). The SEIS investors stay undiluted at 15%; founders move from 40% to 38% each. More attractive to early backers, with the cost borne by the founders.

An existing investor following on adds to a protected position at a fair, de-risked valuation. A live, founder-paid business that reaches a trade sale is better for everyone than a lean one that stalls because its founders could not afford to stay.
